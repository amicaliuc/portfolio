Enterprise F&B Vision

Scope

Product strategy, UX/UI, Prototyping

Role

Design DRI

Year

2024

Square acquired GoParrot in 2022, bringing its Franchise Suite into a payments ecosystem that wasn't built for enterprise F&B complexity. A year into integration, the product still lacked a long-term direction, and competitors like Toast and Olo were already pulling ahead on enterprise.

The problem

Leadership needed a clear answer: what are we building for enterprise sellers, for whom, and in what order? There was no shortage of opinions. Four different teams had roadmaps. Sales had a wishlist. Engineering had tech debt.

The product had grown reactively, adding features without a unifying model for how enterprise clients actually operate at scale. My job was to give the product a north star it didn't have.

My approach

Before touching Figma, I spent weeks synthesizing everything the company already knew: market research, customer interviews, sales feedback, five team roadmaps, a full product audit, and competitive analysis of Toast, Olo, and HungerRush.

The real shift happened when I stopped asking "what features are we missing?" and started asking "why does complexity break for these clients at scale?"

A restaurant with one location has linear relationships: one or two menus, one team, one set of settings. At 20+ locations, those relationships multiply non-linearly. Operators need to push changes at scale while preserving local flexibility.

The shift happened when I stopped asking "what features are we missing?" and started asking "why does complexity break for these clients at scale?"

Without scalability patterns baked into the product's architecture, every new feature would break at enterprise. That reframe changed the entire scope of the problem. The gap wasn't a feature list — it was the absence of a structural model that could hold enterprise complexity.

What I built

From that insight, I designed a vision that covered the five core surfaces where complexity breaks for enterprise operators:

  • Onboarding — a path for multi-location setup.
  • Menu management — a hierarchical publishing model letting operators define menus at the brand, region, or location level.
  • Location management — a groups layer that lets operators treat clusters of locations as units, not individual entries.
  • Reports & AI insights — cross-location rollup views with anomaly detection, replacing location-by-location switching.
  • Permissions — a role-based model built for org structures with layers of access and delegation.

Each surface included high-fidelity UI, prototypes, and a pattern annotation. The final deliverable was a leadership presentation covering the full product vision and a phased transition plan mapping which surfaces to build first and why.

Impact

➀ The vision helped unblock several stalled initiatives ➁ Location groups received funding and launched ➂ Enterprise menus moved forward ➃ Four teams gained a shared language and direction

What made this hard

This initiative didn't start with a brief — I had to create one. That meant defining the problem, earning alignment on the framing, and then designing the solution, all without a PM counterpart. Every design decision had to be defensible as a scalable pattern: something that would still hold at 500-location enterprise scale, not just 20.
